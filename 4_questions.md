
What Branding Structure Would Optimize National Value?
4 SUB-QUESTIONS
1) Is national adoption positive, negative, or tunable?
	Observed (tested): significantly negative under full ACM branding. Population-weighted national follow goes from 15.7% unbranded to 12.4% full ACM (-3.3pp, 95% CI [-5.2, -1.3]). The CI excludes zero — this penalty is not noise.
	Modeled: tunable via architecture, but sensitive to two parameters. The sensitivity matrix crosses neutral-penalty softening (neutral follow-rate drop from -7.1pp toward -4.2pp and -2.5pp) with positive-activation retention (100% → 80% → 60%):
	– Milan-first optimistic (neutral drop -2.5pp, 100% positive retained): +3.0pp [+0.7, +5.4] — significantly positive, but requires full preservation of positive activation
	– Milan-first conservative (neutral drop -2.5pp, 80% positive retained): +1.5pp [-0.6, +3.8] — directionally positive, CI spans zero
	– Moderate softening (neutral drop -4.2pp, 100% positive retained): +0.7pp [-1.5, +3.0] — breakeven, CI spans zero
	– Even moderate softening with slight attenuation (neutral drop -4.2pp, 80% positive): -0.7pp [-2.8, +1.5] — still slightly negative
	The breakeven line runs approximately from (neutral drop -4.2pp, 100% positive) through (neutral drop -2.5pp, ~80% positive). Milan-first is net-positive nationally only if at least ~80% of positive-sentiment activation is retained.
	Caveat: Milan-first was not tested in the survey. The scenarios are counterfactuals constructed from the regression model. The specific magnitudes are assumptions, not measured quantities. The negative interaction is held fixed: hostile identity reactions are likely triggered by the mere association with ACM, not by its visual prominence. If softer branding also reduces negative repulsion, the estimates are conservative.
	Answer: Significantly negative as tested (-3.3pp, CI excludes zero). Potentially tunable to positive under Milan-first, contingent on retaining ≥80% of positive activation while softening the neutral penalty — a specific, empirically testable design challenge. [Revised Sentiment Regression Brief]
2) Is monetization strengthened or weakened?
	National monetization is essentially unchanged under full ACM branding. Population-weighted "any paid" goes 13.4% → 13.3% (-0.1pp, 95% CI [-1.1, +1.0]). The CI spans zero — the effect is not statistically distinguishable from flat. Even at the worst end of the CI, monetization damage is bounded at roughly -1pp nationally.
	Segment-level: ACM fans increase paid-intent materially (+9.5pp ±3.3, significant) while Inter (-3.0pp ±2.6, borderline) and Other Serie A (-1.7pp ±1.8, not significant) decrease, netting to ~flat nationally.
	By paid subcomponents: merch +0.7pp; paid TV and attendance ~flat.
	Why monetization holds despite follow losses: converters have 2× the paid-intent rate of repelled (48.5% vs 24.0%). Branding swaps out lower-quality paid fans for higher-quality ones.
	Sentiment-weighted: +0.3pp (95% CI [-0.7, +1.4]) — also spans zero, confirming flat.
	Answer: Neither meaningfully strengthened nor weakened nationally. Best estimate is flat (-0.1pp), bounded between -1.1pp and +1.0pp at 95% confidence. [Switchers Analysis Brief]
3) Does branding concentrate value in high-LTV segments?
	We don't have LTV but do have paid-intent concentration.
	Paid-intent under branding is highly concentrated among the positive/fan sentiment group: they're 29.1% of population but 80.5% of branded paid-intent.
	Paid-intent deltas by sentiment: Positive/Fan +5.9pp, Neutral -2.2pp, Negative -0.9pp (population-weighted).
	Answer: Yes — branding concentrates paid-intent in the highest-affinity (positive-sentiment) segment. [Switchers Analysis Brief]
4) Can brand architecture mitigate rival repulsion?
	Two layers to answer this:
	Mechanism evidence (tested): repulsion tracks sentiment, not just club
	– Within Inter fans: negative toward ACM -23.2pp ±7.6 (significant), neutral -13.7pp ±6.1 (significant), positive -4.8pp ±12.8 (not significant, n=63).
	– The gradient is clear and the two largest cells are statistically significant. The positive cell is directionally near-zero but the sample is too small (n=63) for a firm conclusion.
	– The same gradient holds within Other Serie A fans (-10.9pp → -11.1pp → -5.0pp) and Non-Fans (-8.1pp → -3.2pp → +1.9pp), though individual sub-group significance varies with cell size.
	– This supports the conclusion that rival repulsion is primarily a sentiment-driven phenomenon, not purely an identity/allegiance effect.
	Architecture mitigation (modeled, not tested)
	– The sensitivity matrix quantifies the design challenge: Milan-first works if the neutral follow-rate drop softens from -7.1pp to about -2.5pp while positive activation stays above ~80%.
	– Negative-sentiment individuals still decline under any architecture (their follow rate drops from 15.6% to ~3.7% regardless of branding softness). Architecture doesn't eliminate repulsion among ACM-hostile fans; it reduces national impact by improving the large neutral block (58% of population).
	– The breakeven is specific and testable through concept testing, naming studies, or A/B brand exposure experiments.
	Answer: Yes for moderates/neutral-adjacent groups — the mechanism evidence is statistically significant and the sensitivity matrix shows a viable path. Hard negatives (~13% of population) still resist under any architecture. The critical unknown — whether Milan-first branding retains ≥80% of positive activation — is the next empirical question. [Revised Sentiment Regression Brief]
